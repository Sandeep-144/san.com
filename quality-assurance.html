Quality Assurance

Introduction to Quality Assurance

Quality Assurance (QA) is a systematic process to determine if a product or service meets specified requirements. It ensures that the product is developed to the highest standards and functions as intended across various environments and use cases.

Principles of Quality Assurance

This section covers the core principles of QA, which include:

  • Prevention over Detection: Emphasizing the prevention of defects during the development process rather than fixing them afterward.
  • Continuous Improvement: Ensuring ongoing refinement of processes and products.
  • Customer Focus: QA ensures that the product meets customer expectations and requirements.
  • Process-Oriented Approach: QA is focused on improving processes to achieve consistent and high-quality results.

Quality Assurance Methodologies

This section discusses various methodologies used in QA, such as:

  • Waterfall Model: A sequential design process, used in software development, where progress is seen as flowing steadily downwards.
  • Agile Testing: An iterative and incremental approach to testing that aligns with agile methodologies like Scrum or Kanban.
  • Test-Driven Development (TDD): A development methodology where testing is performed before writing the actual code.

QA Tools and Automation

This section highlights popular QA tools and automation frameworks:

  • Selenium: A powerful tool for automating web applications.
  • JUnit: A popular testing framework for Java-based applications.
  • TestNG: A testing framework inspired by JUnit but with more powerful features.
  • Jira: Used for bug tracking and project management.
  • Postman: For API testing and automation.

QA Processes

This section explains key QA processes that ensure quality:

  • Test Planning: Outlines the test objectives, resources, and schedule for testing.
  • Test Execution: Running tests to verify that the software meets requirements.
  • Defect Tracking: Identifying and recording defects to ensure they are fixed in a timely manner.
  • Regression Testing: Verifying that recent code changes haven’t introduced new bugs.
